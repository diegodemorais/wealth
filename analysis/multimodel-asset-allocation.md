4. **Diversification benefits:** Combining blocks reduces portfolio volatility through low correlation between factors, DM, and EM. EM and factor tilts both increase expected return but with different risk profiles.

## 2. How Much Factor Tilt is Too Much?

### Recommended Range: 20–30% (moderate tilt)

**Is 25–35% appropriate?**  
Yes, for a long-horizon investor, 25–35% is reasonable but on the higher end. The key is that factor tilts should not dominate the portfolio—maintaining exposure to broad market beta is crucial for diversification and liquidity.

**Risks of Over-Tilting (>40%):**

1. **Factor crowding and reversal:** As factor investing grows popular, premiums may compress (Shalit & Yitzhaki 2021). Overweighting factors increases vulnerability to prolonged underperformance (e.g., value underperformed 2007–2019).

2. **Tracking error and behavioral risk:** High factor tilts increase deviation from the market, leading to longer periods of underperformance. Retail investors may abandon the strategy during drawdowns, realizing losses.

3. **Implementation costs:** Factor-tilted ETFs often have higher expense ratios (0.15–0.40% vs. 0.05–0.10% for cap-weighted) and higher turnover (especially small-cap and value), eroding returns.

4. **Concentration risk:** Some factor strategies (e.g., small-cap) have higher exposure to specific risks (e.g., financial distress).

**Academic Sources:**

## 3. EM Allocation: Academically Supported Range

### Recommended Range: 15–25% (vs. 10–12% in global cap-weighted)

**Arguments for 15–25% EM:**

1. **Higher expected returns:** Dimson, Marsh, Staunton (2020) show EM outperformed DM by ~3% annually (nominal) over 1900–2020, though with higher volatility. Over a 51-year horizon, this compounds significantly.

2. **Long-horizon time diversification:** EM returns are more volatile, but longer horizons reduce the probability of underperformance. For a 51-year horizon, EM's higher mean returns dominate.

3. **Diversification:** EM has low correlation with DM (0.5–0.7), improving portfolio efficiency.

**Arguments for 10–12% (global cap-weighted):**

1. **EM risk premium may be fading:** Rapid EM index inclusion has reduced the "growth" premium. Some research (e.g., Grobys 2021) suggests the EM premium is time-varying.

2. **Political and currency risks:** EM exposure introduces country-specific risks (e.g., Brazil, China) that may not be compensated.

3. **Simplicity:** Global cap-weighted requires no ongoing allocation decisions.

**Evidence:**  
- The MSCI ACWI Index has ~12% EM weight (as of 2024).  
- Research from Vanguard (2022) suggests 15–20% EM for long-horizon investors seeking higher returns.

**Academic Sources:**
- Dimson, E., Marsh, P. & Staunton, M. (2020). *Triumph of the Optimists*. Princeton University Press.
- Vanguard (2022). "Strategic Asset Allocation: Global Perspectives."

## 4. Key Synthesis: Factor/EM Tilt vs. Simple Cap-Weighted

### Recommendation: **Meaningful tilt toward factors and EM is warranted for this profile.**

**Rationale:**

1. **Long horizon aligns with factor and EM premiums:** Both premiums require time to materialize and are more reliable over 10+ year periods. With 51 years, the investor can afford the higher short-term volatility.

2. **Evidence supports modest outperformance:** Factor tilts and EM overweighting have historically added 1–3% annualized real return. Over 51 years, this compounds to a ~60–150% increase in terminal wealth.

3. **Implementation is feasible with low-cost ETFs:** Brazilian investors can access international ETFs (e.g., iShares, Vanguard) with reasonable costs (0.10–0.30%).

**Counterargument (simplicity wins):**

- After costs, factor/EM tilts may underperform due to implementation drag.
- Behavioral risk: investors may abandon the strategy during underperformance periods.
- Academic evidence is sample-specific; future premiums may be lower.

For a disciplined, long-horizon investor, a portfolio with **55% Block A, 25% Block B, 20% Block C** offers a reasonable balance of expected return enhancement and diversification. This is simpler than a fully optimized portfolio but captures most of the academic alpha.
